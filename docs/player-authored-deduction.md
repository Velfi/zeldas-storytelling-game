# Player-Authored Deduction

The central design goal is for the player to construct and defend the solution, not recognize it from a prescribed list of answers. Investigation therefore awards small, reusable facts—where someone was seen, how an object moved, when an event occurred, or what a physical test proved—without announcing the conclusion those facts imply. Evidence may support several plausible hypotheses, and the player must decide how discoveries connect into a causal account of who acted, what happened, where and when it occurred, and how the crime was concealed.

The theory board turns that interpretation into play. Players arrange discovered facts into claims, order events, compare evidence, and stage physical reconstructions in the dollhouse. The game evaluates the resulting theory by testing chronology, feasibility, contradictions, and evidentiary support. Its feedback should identify a precise weakness—such as an impossible journey, an unsupported assumption, or evidence left unexplained—without replacing the player's choices or repairing the theory automatically. Incorrect constructions remain intact so they can be reconsidered and revised.

This approach provides authorship without requiring unrestricted free-form input. The case still supplies authored evidence, propositions, and validation rules, but the player controls which facts matter, what they mean together, and when the argument is strong enough to become an accusation. A successful conclusion is therefore earned through a coherent chain of demonstrated claims rather than a correct dialogue selection; the canonical reconstruction is reserved for the post-case explanation, after the player has committed to an interpretation of their own.
