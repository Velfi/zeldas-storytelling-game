# Design Principles

## Priority order

Resolve conflicts in this order:

1. Canonical mystery truth and required physical evidence
2. Reachability, interaction, circulation, and safety
3. Room function and occupant behavior
4. Composition, style, and atmosphere
5. Decorative richness

## Functional composition

- Give each room one primary purpose and at most a few supporting purposes.
- Establish a dominant focal point and subordinate elements; avoid equal visual emphasis everywhere.
- Place large anchors first, then form activity groups around them.
- Orient seats toward their social or visual purpose, not automatically toward walls.
- Keep frequently used objects within plausible reach of the activity that needs them.
- Treat negative space as circulation, pause, framing, or evidence—not unused area to fill.
- Vary density by zone. Even spacing and uniform wall-hugging usually read as procedural.

## Circulation and interaction

- Trace routes from each entrance to every primary activity and required interaction.
- Preserve door swings, opening approaches, chair pullback, cabinet access, and inspection positions.
- Keep mystery-critical objects reachable and visually distinguishable without spotlighting their conclusion.
- Check the design from doorway, route, seated/activity, and interaction viewpoints.

## Occupant modeling

Derive choices from specific causes:

- wealth controls quality, age, repair, and replacement patterns;
- habits control reach, clustering, storage, and wear;
- taste controls palette, display, symmetry, and ornament;
- relationships control photographs, gifts, shared seating, and exclusions;
- recent events control displacement, absence, interruption, concealment, and cleanup.

Prefer one strong, consistent occupant model over a generic style label.

## Lived-in detail

Use restrained irregularity:

- incomplete or inherited sets;
- wear near repeated movement and touch;
- temporary objects near current activities;
- personal collections with storage logic;
- repaired, replaced, or missing items;
- slight misalignment caused by use;
- clean or empty areas with an explainable cause.

Avoid random scatter, pervasive clutter, arbitrary rotations, and anomalies with no causal explanation.

## Mystery staging

Separate normal state from event state. For each irregularity, know:

- what was normally present and where;
- who changed it;
- when and why it changed;
- who could observe the change;
- what conclusion it may support;
- whether it must survive gameplay interactions.

Preserve authored sightlines, travel paths, hiding capacity, access restrictions, and reconstruction positions. A beautiful change that invalidates physical reasoning is a failed change.

## Lighting intent

Compose lighting in roles:

- ambient light establishes readable baseline and time of day;
- task light supports work, reading, preparation, or examination;
- accent light reinforces focal hierarchy and atmosphere;
- practical fixtures should correspond to plausible occupant behavior.

Do not use lighting to accidentally reveal hidden content, obscure essential evidence, or contradict authored time and power state.

## Critique checklist

Ask:

- Can the room's function and occupant be inferred without exposition?
- Does the doorway view establish hierarchy quickly?
- Are activity groups spatially coherent?
- Is the primary route legible and unobstructed?
- Is there a reason for each conspicuous object or empty area?
- Does repetition look intentional rather than catalog-limited?
- Are asymmetry and imperfection caused by life or story?
- Are clues discoverable through investigation rather than decorative emphasis?
- Does every event-state anomaly preserve the canonical reasoning chain?
